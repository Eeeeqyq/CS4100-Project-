Datasets for context-aware music mood prediction
The biggest challenge isn't finding data — it's that almost no single public dataset fully connects situational context to music mood preference at scale. Your best path forward combines a large-scale listening dataset that has partial context (#nowplaying-RS, with 11.6 million events, timestamps, geolocation, and Spotify audio features) with a mood-labeling dataset (MTG-Jamendo, with 56 mood/theme tags across 55,000 tracks) to construct a supervised classification target. SiTunes remains your richest single source for the biometric→preference signal, but supplementary datasets can dramatically expand your training data and validate the HMM-to-classifier pipeline you're designing.
This report organizes 27 datasets into four functional categories: direct context+preference datasets, large-scale listening data enrichable with context, mood/emotion label sources for defining prediction targets, and adjacent-domain physiological datasets for your HMM component. Each section highlights what's downloadable now, how it fits your architecture, and which datasets pair together.

Direct context-to-preference datasets: small but gold-standard
These are the only public datasets that directly link situational context to music preference outcomes. All are small, which is precisely why you need the supplementary strategy described later.
SiTunes (~2,000 listening logs, 30 users, 300+ tracks) remains your best single dataset. It captures heart rate, activity intensity, step count, and activity type from a smart wristband; environmental context including time of day, weather, and rescaled GPS coordinates; and emotional self-reports of valence and arousal both before and after listening. Music ratings provide the preference outcome. The three-stage study design (lab baseline → field with standard recommender → field with situational recommender) even gives you natural experimental variation. Available on GitHub from the CHIIR 2024 paper. For your HMM approach, the sequential biometric signals (heart rate, activity) are the input sequence, and valence/arousal shift plus rating are the targets.
InCarMusic (~4,000 ratings, 42 users, 139 tracks across 10 genres) provides 8 contextual dimensions — driving style, road type, landscape, sleepiness, traffic conditions, mood, weather, and natural phenomena — paired with 1–5 scale music ratings. It's context-dense but note that conditions are assumed/self-reported rather than sensor-measured. Download via the CARSKit repository on GitHub or on Kaggle. While it lacks biometric signals, the 8-dimensional context space is useful for training the neural classifier component of your pipeline on a richer feature set than SiTunes alone provides.
PMEmo (794 songs, 457 subjects) pairs electrodermal activity (EDA) recordings during music listening with both static and dynamic (0.5-second interval) valence-arousal annotations. This is the closest thing to a large-sample physiological→music emotion dataset. The EDA signal can directly feed your HMM as a continuous physiological observation. Available on GitHub and Google Drive.
DEAP (32 participants × 40 music videos = 1,280 trials) captures 32-channel EEG plus 13 peripheral signals (including heart rate, skin conductance, respiration, and temperature) during music video watching, with ratings for arousal, valence, dominance, and liking. Requires an EULA from Queen Mary University of London. While the stimulus is music video rather than pure audio, the physiological-to-preference mapping is directly transferable.

Large-scale listening datasets you can enrich with context
No large-scale dataset ships with biometric context, but several include enough metadata (timestamps, geolocation, user demographics) to reconstruct contextual features. The enrichment strategy: overlay historical weather data via APIs, derive time-of-day and day-of-week features from timestamps, and use Spotify audio features to assign mood labels to tracks.
#nowplaying-RS is the strongest candidate for your project. It contains 11.6 million listening events from 139,000 users and 346,000 tracks, collected from Twitter #nowplaying tweets. Critically, it already includes Spotify audio features (valence, energy, danceability, tempo, instrumentalness, etc.) for each track — meaning you can derive mood buckets directly from valence/energy quadrants without any external join. Contextual dimensions include timestamps, user timezone, geolocation (as GeoJSON coordinates for geolocated tweets), tweet language as a cultural proxy, and 5,290 hashtags with assigned sentiment scores that serve as implicit mood indicators. Licensed CC BY 4.0 on Zenodo (DOI: 10.5281/zenodo.3248543). For your classifier, this dataset provides: time-of-day + day-of-week from timestamps, approximate location/culture from timezone and coordinates, implicit mood from hashtag sentiment, and a naturally defined mood-bucket target from Spotify's valence and energy features.
LFM-2b (2 billion listening events, 120,000+ users, 50 million tracks, spanning 2005–2020) is the largest music listening dataset available. It includes timestamps, user demographics (country, gender, age), fine-grained genre/style labels, and user-generated tags (including mood tags). While it lacks direct contextual signals, the timestamps plus user country enable weather enrichment via historical weather APIs (e.g., Visual Crossing or Open-Meteo). Music items link to MusicBrainz IDs, enabling joins to AcousticBrainz audio features and MTG-Jamendo mood labels. Available from JKU Linz.
Spotify MSSD (~160 million sessions, 3.7 million tracks) captures session-level interaction patterns — skips, pauses, completions — plus audio features and hour-of-day. The skip behavior is an implicit preference signal: completed songs indicate positive preference. Available on AICrowd. For your HMM, sequential skip/listen patterns within a session could serve as an observable sequence encoding evolving preference state.
KKBOX (millions of events, from WSDM Cup 2018 on Kaggle) includes user demographics (city, age, gender), song metadata (genre, language, ISRC-derived country/year), and source UI context (discovery vs. search vs. library). The task is re-listen prediction, which maps well to preference classification. The source context features (how the user found the song) add a unique contextual dimension not found elsewhere.

Mood and emotion label datasets for defining prediction targets
Your classification target — "predict preferred music mood bucket" — requires a mapping from tracks to mood categories. These datasets provide that mapping, and several pair directly with the listening data above via shared identifiers.
MTG-Jamendo is the most comprehensive option: 55,000+ full audio tracks tagged with 56 mood/theme labels (happy, sad, energetic, dark, romantic, epic, etc.) plus 87 genre tags and 40 instrument tags. The mood/theme subset covers 18,486 tracks. Audio is freely available under Creative Commons licenses; metadata is CC BY-NC-SA 4.0. Available on Zenodo and via the MTG GitHub. For your project, these 56 mood tags can be clustered into 4–6 mood buckets (e.g., using Russell's circumplex model quadrants: happy/energetic, calm/relaxed, sad/melancholic, angry/tense) to define your classification target.
DEAM (1,802 tracks with continuous valence-arousal annotations at per-second resolution) provides dimensional emotion labels that map directly to mood quadrants. Annotations are on a 1–9 scale for both valence and arousal. Free for non-profit research from the University of Geneva. The per-second dynamic annotations are particularly useful if you want to study how mood evolves within a track.
MuSe (90,001 songs with valence, arousal, and dominance scores derived from Last.fm tags) is the largest mood-labeled dataset with Spotify IDs, enabling direct linking to #nowplaying-RS tracks. Available via the Journal of Open Humanities Data.
Spotify audio features on Kaggle — multiple community datasets (278K–600K tracks) map Spotify's valence and energy features to mood categories. While these live in a licensing gray area (Spotify TOS technically prohibits redistribution of API data), they're practically useful. Valence and energy together define a natural mood quadrant: high valence + high energy = happy/excited, high valence + low energy = calm/relaxed, low valence + high energy = angry/tense, low valence + low energy = sad/melancholic. Since #nowplaying-RS already contains these features, you can derive mood buckets without any external dataset.
Deezer Mood Detection Dataset (18,644 tracks with valence/arousal values, linking to Million Song Dataset IDs) and WASABI (2 million songs with NLP-derived emotion from lyrics, CC BY-NC-SA 4.0 on Zenodo) provide additional mood labeling at different scales and from different modalities (acoustic vs. lyrical emotion).

Adjacent-domain datasets for the HMM biometric pipeline
Your architecture uses an HMM to encode sequential biometric signals into a belief state. These datasets provide the physiological signal → affective state mapping your HMM needs to learn, even though they're not music-preference datasets.
WESAD (15 subjects, chest + wrist wearable data including BVP, ECG, EDA, EMG, respiration, temperature, and 3-axis accelerometry) maps three affective states — neutral, stress, and amusement — from wearable signals. The Empatica E4 wrist data is consumer-grade wearable data closely matching what a real music app would access. Available on UCI Machine Learning Repository under a public license. This is ideal for pretraining your HMM's physiological state encoder: the observation model learned from WESAD (biometric sequence → stress/amusement/neutral) transfers to the music context, where stress might predict preference for calming music.
CASE (30 participants, continuous valence-arousal annotation via joystick with synchronized ECG, BVP, EMG, GSR, respiration, and skin temperature at 1,000 Hz) provides the highest-quality continuous physio→emotion mapping available. Licensed CC BY 4.0 on Figshare. The continuous annotation means you have a dense time series of emotion labels aligned with physiological signals — perfect training data for an HMM where hidden states represent emotional regions and observations are biometric features.
LifeSnaps (71 participants, 4+ months, 71 million rows of Fitbit Sense data including heart rate, steps, sleep stages, skin temperature, SpO2, and EDA-based stress scores, plus ecological momentary assessments of mood and Big Five personality scores) is the largest in-the-wild wearable dataset with mood labels. Licensed openly on Zenodo. For your project, the daily patterns of (heart rate + activity + sleep + stress score) → self-reported mood provide a naturalistic context-to-affect model. Personality traits can serve as static user features in your neural classifier.
ExtraSensory (60 users, 300,000+ minutes, phone accelerometer/gyroscope/magnetometer at 40 Hz plus location and audio) labels 51 activity/context categories — crucially including "listening to music" as a label. Available free from UCSD. This dataset lets you build the activity-recognition component: phone sensor data → activity state (running, studying, commuting, socializing), which then feeds into your neural classifier alongside the HMM's affective belief state.
StudentLife (48–200+ students over 10 weeks, with automatic sensing of activity, sleep, conversation, and mobility, plus ecological momentary assessments of stress and mood via the PAM photographic affect meter) provides longitudinal context-to-mood mappings. Publicly available from Dartmouth. The stress/mood/activity trajectories over weeks demonstrate how situational context evolves — relevant for modeling temporal dependencies in your HMM.


Conclusion
The dataset landscape for context-aware music preference prediction is fragmented by design — no single study has captured large-scale biometric data alongside music listening at the scale needed for deep learning. Your HMM+classifier architecture is actually well-suited to this fragmentation, because it naturally decomposes into a biometric encoder (trainable on small physiological datasets like WESAD, CASE, and SiTunes) and a preference classifier (trainable on large contextual datasets like #nowplaying-RS).
